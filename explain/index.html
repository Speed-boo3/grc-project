What is GRC?
GRC stands for Governance, Risk and Compliance. It is the strategic layer of cybersecurity. While technical security teams deal with day-to-day threats, GRC makes sure the organisation has the right structure, knows its risks and can prove its controls are working. Almost every organisation above a certain size has a GRC function, and demand for people who understand it is growing fast.
🏛️
Governance
Who is responsible? What are the rules?
Governance is about setting the rules and making sure people follow them. This includes writing security policies, defining roles and responsibilities, and making sure leadership is involved in security decisions. Without governance, security has no strategy behind it.
⚠️
Risk
What could go wrong? How bad?
Risk management is about systematically identifying threats, scoring them by likelihood and impact, and deciding what to do about them. You cannot fix everything at once, so risk scoring tells you what to prioritise.
✅
Compliance
Are controls working? Can we prove it?
Compliance means meeting the requirements of a framework or regulation like ISO 27001 or GDPR. It is not enough to have a policy that says the right thing. You need to show, in an audit, that you actually do what the policy says.
📄
Security Policy
A written document that defines the rules everyone must follow. It covers access control, data handling, incident reporting and more. Without a policy, there is no baseline to measure against in an audit.
📊
Risk Register
A live document tracking all identified risks, their scores and what is being done about them. It is the central artifact of risk management and is reviewed regularly by management.
🔍
Gap Analysis
A comparison between where you are now and where a framework says you should be. The gaps become your remediation roadmap. The starting point for any compliance programme.
🛡️
Control
A safeguard or countermeasure. Controls can be technical (a firewall), organisational (a policy) or physical (a lock). ISO 27001 has 93 of them. Your job in GRC is to select, implement and prove they work.
⚖️
Risk Treatment
What you decide to do about a risk. You can mitigate it (add controls), transfer it (buy insurance), avoid it (stop doing the risky thing) or accept it (document the decision and move on).
📋
Audit
A formal review of your controls by an internal or external auditor. They check whether your documented processes match what actually happens. GRC work is largely about being audit-ready at all times.

Compliance frameworks
Frameworks are structured sets of security controls that organisations use as a benchmark. They tell you what good security looks like. GRC analysts work with frameworks daily — selecting controls, assessing gaps and preparing for audits.
ISO 27001 is the international standard for information security management. Organisations can get certified by passing an external audit. It is the most widely recognised security certification in the world and appears in almost every GRC job description.
Organisational controls
Covers security policies, roles and responsibilities, risk management, supplier relationships and how security is governed at the management level. This is the foundation everything else builds on.
People controls
Covers screening new employees, security awareness training, disciplinary processes for violations and what happens when someone leaves the organisation. People are the most common attack vector.
Physical controls
Covers building access, equipment protection, clean desk policies and physical media. Often overlooked in technical security work but essential for a complete security programme.
Technological controls
Covers authentication, access rights, encryption, logging, vulnerability management, network security and software development practices. The largest category with 34 controls.
NIST CSF is the US National Institute of Standards and Technology Cybersecurity Framework. Version 2.0 was released in 2024 and added a sixth function: Govern. It is flexible, widely used and appears in almost all US-based GRC roles.
Govern — know your strategy
New in CSF 2.0. Covers organisational context, risk management strategy, roles and responsibilities, and cybersecurity policies. Sets the strategic direction for everything else.
Identify — know your assets
Understand your assets, business environment and risks. You cannot protect what you do not know about. Asset inventories and risk assessments live here.
Protect — put safeguards in place
Access control, training, data security, platform security and technology resilience. The controls that reduce the impact of an incident.
Detect — find threats that get through
Continuous monitoring, anomaly detection and alerting. The earlier you detect an incident, the less damage it causes.
Respond — take action
Incident response planning, communications, analysis and containment. What happens once a threat is confirmed.
Recover — get back to normal
Recovery planning, improvements and communications. Restoring capabilities after an incident and learning from what happened.
GDPR is the EU General Data Protection Regulation. It applies to any organisation that processes data about EU residents, regardless of where the organisation is based. Non-compliance can result in fines of up to 4% of global annual revenue.
Lawful basis for processing
Every time you collect or use personal data, you need a lawful reason. Consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task or legitimate interests. Documenting this is a GRC responsibility.
Data subject rights
Individuals have the right to access their data, correct it, delete it (right to be forgotten) and move it elsewhere (data portability). Organisations must be able to respond to these requests within 30 days.
Breach notification
If personal data is breached, you must notify the relevant data protection authority within 72 hours of becoming aware. If the breach is likely to cause harm to individuals, you must also notify them directly.
Data Protection Officer (DPO)
Certain organisations are required to appoint a DPO. They oversee GDPR compliance, advise on data protection impact assessments and act as the contact point for supervisory authorities.

How sensitive data is stored, transmitted and disposed of. Covers encryption requirements and retention schedules.

ISO 27001 compliance checklist
Work through this checklist to see how compliant a typical organisation is against ISO 27001. Each item is a real control from the standard. In a real audit, every unchecked item is a finding that needs to be addressed.
0 / 12 controls passed
  • Information security policies are documented and approved
    The organisation has written security policies that have been reviewed and approved by management. Without this, you have no governance baseline.
  • Roles and responsibilities for security are defined
    Everyone knows who is responsible for what. Security cannot be everyone's responsibility and nobody's responsibility at the same time.
  • A risk assessment has been conducted
    Risks have been identified, scored and documented in a risk register. This is the foundation of ISO 27001 — everything flows from understanding your risks.
  • Access control policy is implemented
    Users only have access to what they need. Access is reviewed regularly and removed promptly when no longer needed.
  • Cryptography is used for sensitive data
    Sensitive data is encrypted at rest and in transit. Cryptographic keys are managed securely.
  • Security awareness training is provided to all staff
    All employees receive regular security training. People are still the most common attack vector and training is one of the most effective controls.
  • Vulnerability management is in place
    Systems are scanned for vulnerabilities regularly and patches are applied within defined timelines. Unpatched systems are one of the most exploited attack surfaces.
  • Logging and monitoring is active
    Security events are logged and reviewed. Log integrity is protected. Without logs, you cannot detect or investigate incidents.
  • An incident response plan exists and has been tested
    The organisation knows what to do when an incident occurs. The plan has been rehearsed, not just written down.
  • Supplier security is managed
    Third-party suppliers who handle your data or have access to your systems are assessed for security. Supply chain attacks are a major risk vector.
  • Business continuity and recovery plans exist
    The organisation can recover from a significant incident. Backups are taken regularly and tested. Recovery time objectives are defined.
  • Compliance with legal and regulatory requirements is monitored
    The organisation tracks relevant laws, regulations and standards. Changes are identified and acted on. GDPR, sector-specific rules and contractual obligations are included.
